Interactive Map of Tweets Geographic Scopes

Morteza Karimzadeh

Purpose:

It is difficult to quantify how – and to what extent - the public engages with events in other countries. Twitter users all over the globe post more than 500 million tweets every day[1]. They also discuss places in their tweets. Therefore, Twitter provides a lens through which geographic research can investigate public discourse as it relates to place. This interactive map is a pilot study to visualize relations between places mentioned in Twitter posts and places where users live. It helps identify whether Twitter users in different countries engage more with domestic or international (or transnational) events. It also visualizes the extent to which places in other countries are being discussed through online platforms/social media.

Broader impacts:

This visualization has implications for the design of algorithms attempting to automatically geolocate places mentioned in tweets for use in sentiment or spatial analysis, situational awareness, and advertisement.  Most place names are ambiguous and refer to more than one location. For example, London can refer to London, Texas or London in England. This type of visualization helps gauge whether Twitter users’ profile locations can be used to disambiguate places that are mentioned in their tweets.

Data processing and visualization:

We used the Twitter Streaming Application Programming Interface (API) to collect close to one billion tweets from January 1, 2014 to January 1, 2016. We used keywords to filter for tweets based upon relatively current events, mainly related to crisis events. From this collection, a random sample of 6000 tweets was drawn by querying for 700 tweets for each of the 12 event-related terms including earthquake, Ebola, fire, flood, flu, malaria, measles, protest, rebels, riot, tornado, violence. These keywords were selected from three kinds of events, representing (a) natural disasters, (b) infectious disease, and (c) human threats/violence, for which we expected to have a higher number of tweets that are place-focused than in an “average” sample (e.g., those about celebrities).

In order to produce a more spatially and temporally distributed sample of tweets, a stratified random selection of tweets was performed, with temporal stratification per month. We ensured only tweets that are primarily in English were selected and that no two selected tweets are the same or very similar (e.g. one is a re-tweet of another). We replaced similar (re)tweets and deleted tweets and, to keep the distribution across keywords, added more tweets from the database. The resulting 6,711 tweets were manually geoannotated using an annotation software platform, meaning that place names in a tweeter’s (user’s) profile location and place names in the tweet’s text were given geographic locations[2].

The resulting 2185 tweets that had place names in them were processed to establish a link between every place listed in the profile location (by the tweeter) and every place mentioned in the tweet text. A list of connection triples in the form of [source, target, count of connecting tweets] was formed for data visualization.

Map Use:

The interactive map uses open source JavaScript libraries of leaflet, D3, and relies heavily on SpatialSankey to visualize the links on the map. Yellow circles show the proportion of tweets mentioning places within the same country and purple circles indicate the proportion of tweets discussing places located in other countries. Holding the mouse over each country’s yellow circle draws links to other countries that are mentioned by users located inside the selected country. Link thickness indicates the proportion of tweets mentioning linked country from the source country. Links appearances can be modified using the boxes on the right side of the map.
